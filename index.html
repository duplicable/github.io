Research

Estimating Behavioral Learning Equilibria in DSGE Models, 2017

(Joint with C. Hommes and K. Mavromatis )

Adaptive learning algorithms in macroeconomics are rising in popularity as a plausible alternative to the Rational Expectations (RE) paradigm. Introduction of these algorithms into operational macroeconometric models is known to generate desirable time-series properties (such as persitence and volatility amplification), yet evidence on their empirical performance of these models under adaptive learning still remains scarce. In the context of DSGE models, the aim of this paper is to explore a simple misspecification equilibrium resulting from an underparameterized adaptive learning rule. In order to estimate the resulting models, we construct a framework that allows us to re-cast the underlying non-linear problem as a sequence of linear VARs. Applying our methodology to two well-known models shows that the results substantially improve relative to the RE counterpart in terms of likelihood and forecasting performance.

Results of a Massive Experiment on Virtual Currency Endowments and Money Demand, 2017 *

(Joint with E. Castronova , N. Zivic, I. Andjelkovic and M. Dekic., published in PLoS One .)

We use a month-long experiment with more than a half-million participants to test the effects of virtual currency endowments on player retention and virtual currency demand. An increase in endowments of a virtual currency should lower the demand for the currency in the short run. However, in the long run, we would expect money demand to rise in response to inflation in the virtual world. We test for this behavior in a virtual field experiment in Top 11, one of Europe's largest online soccer management games. Our results indicate that player money purchases are significantly higher in the shards where higher endowments were given. This suggests that a positive change in the money supply in a virtual context leads to inflation and increased money demand, and does so much more quickly than in real-world economies. These results confirm that it is possible to use a virtual world to mimic real-world behavior on a grand scale, which opens the door to the predictive use of game worlds in policymaking, commercial and other real-life applications.
